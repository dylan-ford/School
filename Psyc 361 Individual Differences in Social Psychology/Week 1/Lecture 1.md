What is personality?
- enduring characteristics and behaviour that comprise a person's unique adjustment to includes major traits, interests, drives, values, self-concept, abilities, and emotional patterns
- most personality theories agree 3 cores features of personality:
	- exist along a continuum (normal distribution)
	- exhibit temporal stability (stable across life)
	- exhibit cross-situational consistency ()
1. Normal distribution of traits
	- most people are about average in any given trait
	- lower and higher levels of any trait can have certain strengths/disadvantages
	- many modern psychological theories refelct an origin in the study and treatment of "disturbed behaviour"
	- continuity assumption: there is no discontinuity between "disturbed" or abnormal behaviour and behaviour we consider normal
2. Temporal stability
	- most personality traits are fairly stable over the developmental trajectory
3. Cross-situational consistency
	- one major debate in personality psychology concerns the relative power of personality vs situations as predictors of behaviour
	- Person x situation interaction: acknowledges that while peoples personality traits may mostly be stable across situations/time, there may be some situations that alter people's expression of personality and behaviour
- utility
	- personality is often used to refer to our impressions of ourselves or someone else
	- although this provides interesting data about our judgements, they are not useful in understanding origins of human action
	- personality psychology is often interested in understanding predictors of people's behaviours
Trait theory
- single-trait approach
	- what do people with a certain personality trait do?
		- examines correlations between one trait and many behaviours
		- how does high narcissism impact cognition? affect? interpersonal consequences?
- many-trait approach
	- different traits that predict a single behaviour
		- what traits predict engagement in prosocial/volunteering behaviour?
	- correlation between one behaviour and many traits
- typological approach
	- concept of distinguishing people by their behavioural traits, but according to "types" or "categories"
	- often represented as opposing pairs; belonging to one discreet group or another
	- example: Myers Briggs
		- based on typologies from Jung
		- sorts people into 16 categories based on 4 dichotomies
		- separates traits into dichotomies which in reality are not mutually-exclusive
			- doesn't reflect bell curve of traits
		- poor validity, reliability
- essential-trait approach
	- aims to find the most important cluster of traits that can explain a significant portion of a person's behaviour
Big 5
- fundamental dimensions of personality
	- cross-cultural evidence replicates the 5 traits
	- actually predicts behavioural tendencies
	- shows strong heritability
- Openness
	- intellectual curiosity, preference for variety and new experiences, active imagination, not dogmatic, aesthetic sensitivity
	- tends to decrease as we age
- Conscientiousness
	- responsible, dependable, self-disciplined, persistent, organized and structured
- Extraversion
	- sociable, enjoys social interaction, active, assertive, likes excitement and stimulation 
- Agreeableness
	- cooperative, empathetic, altruistic, places greater value on relationships
- Neuroticism
	- volatile negative emotions; more likely to experience anger, anxiety, embarrassment, disgust, jealousy
- these traits are relatively uncorrelated with each other, meaning they can occur in all combinations
- a particular score on one trait may manifest differently depending on a person's scores on other traits
