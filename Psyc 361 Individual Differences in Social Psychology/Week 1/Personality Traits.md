- reflect people's characteristic patterns of thoughts, feelings, and behaviours
- trait psychology: reflects the idea that people differ from one another on basic trait dimensions that persist across time and situations
- 5 factor model: Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism
	- critics argue people differ greatly between situations; trait vs situation debate
	- scores on any trait in the big 5 have little overlap with any other trait
- personality traits reflect continuous distributions and do not prescribe discrete personality types
- three criteria that characterize personality traits:
	- consistency
		- individuals must be somewhat consistent in their behaviours across situations
	- stability
		- individuals must be somewhat stable over time in behaviours related to a trait
	- individual differences
- Lexical hypothesis: all important personality characteristics should be reflected in the language we use to describe other people
	- resulted in removing synonyms of personality characteristics into what we now have as the 5 factor model
- traits describe stable, persisting patterns of behaviour, which can have broad-ranging consequences in areas of our lives
Sub traits
- each of the big 5 traits can appear differently in different settings, and an individual scoring high in a particular score can present as if they score low in different situations
- there are different ways of displaying each of the 5 personality scores, called facets
- Traits provide a broad, likely summary of expected behaviour, facets provide a more nuanced, accurate description
HEXACO model:
- adds honesty-humility as a sixth dimension of personality; people high in this traits are sincere, modest, and fair
Person-situation debate
- Walter Mischel published a book in the 60s that found little consistency in behaviours across situations in many instances
- proposed psychologists should study peoples' reactions to situations over assuming stability of traits
	- e.g. some children may cheat when the risk of being caught is low and the reward is high, others may enjoy the thrill and still cheat even if the risk is high
- thus behaviour itself results from a persons' unique evaluation of the risks and rewards present at a single moment + their evaluation of their abilities and values
- Mischel argued that behaviours are driven by:
	- the interaction between very specific, psychologically meaningful features of a situation people are in
	- the person's unique way of perceiving the situation
	- their abilities for dealing with it

- research has found that on average the effect of the situation on behaviour is about as strong as personality traits 
	- personality traits indicate how people will act on average but are not good at predicting how people will act at a given moment in a specific situation
