- student academic success is determined not only through content knowledge and cognitive ability but also beliefs, attitudes, and values
- students holding the belief that intelligence is a stable, unchanging characteristic are more likely to interpret struggle and failure as an indication that they are not intellectually capable of succeeding 
	- resulting in avoiding challenges, quitting when encountering hardship, and less academic achievement
- growth mindset: belief that intelligence is a changeable trait, improved through effort and guidance
- mindset affects goals, how students attribute success/failure, and coping mechanisms
factors influencing mindset
- how educators and parents praise children communicates implicit messages about the nature of intelligence
	- praising attributes > success results from innate traits and encourages fixed mindset
	- praising process > implies success is due to behaviours and encourages growth mindset
	- greater academic success > stronger growth mindset in subsequent years (May not be generalizable outside of the Greek middle school student cohort)
- trajectory of mindset change was related to their experience with academic struggle, with difficulty shifted towards fixed and success shifted towards growth
	- findings that first year undergrad students tend to shift toward fixed mindsets
5 factors students describe as shaping their mindsets:
- academic experiences
- observing peers
- deducing logically
- taking societal cues
- formal learning

- different student populations when talking about which factors influenced their beliefs on mindset stated: that how authority figures talk about intelligence is still influential and also - own experiences and observations affect mindset beliefs
- this could indicate that factors influencing mindset may change over time from reliance on authority figures to own experience
- mindset beliefs and academic performance influence each other
