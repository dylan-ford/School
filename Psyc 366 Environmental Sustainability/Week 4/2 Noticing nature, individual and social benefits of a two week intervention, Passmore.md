• What did Passmore et al do in this study?
- randomly assigned participants to either nature, human-built, or business-as-usual groups. Participants in human-built and nature groups were told to pay attention to how their respective objects in their environment and write down the emotions they invoked over 2 weeks 
• What effects did they find on different outcomes?
- nature group showed significantly higher levels of net positive affect, elevating experiences, general sense of connectedness, and prosocial orientation compared to other groups
• In terms of size, how do those effects compare to other positive psychology interventions?
- effect size (0.29 to 0.49) were at the high end/larger than the average effect size for positive psychology interventions
• What was it about the experimental manipulation (and what people were doing) that made
the most difference? 
- paying attention to and noticing nature in everyday routines.
• Are their alternative explanations that were ruled out? 
- the study ruled out the idea that participants needed to spend more time in nature to see benefits, as no groups spent a significantly longer/shorter time in nature in any condition
- they also ruled out individual differences as individual trait levels of nature connectedness didnt affect reported benefits
• What themes did the authors find for participants in nature condition?
- nature photos were significantly more associated with feelings of awe, freedom, hope, peacefulness, and rejuvenation
• What were participants surprised about?
- participants were surprised by how much and to what degree nature affected their emotions
