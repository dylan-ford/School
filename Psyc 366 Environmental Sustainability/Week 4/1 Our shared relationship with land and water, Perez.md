Abstract
- indigenous knowledge systems view the world as an interdependent unity of beings within their environment
- Mayangna (Nicaragua)
	- health of their community is tied to that of mother earth
	- struggle for enforcement of existing laws which prevent deforestation
- Anishinaabe (Kettle and Stony point first nations)
	- water is essential for the preservation of a traditional and contemporary way of life
- Codified in indigenous knowledge systems are teachings for balanced relationships with creation which secures sustainability for future generations
- This paper calls for holistic and participatory perspectives to governance, and recognizing the spiritual and cultural relationships with water

- western colonization has disrupted indigenous ways of life and their relationship to the earth
Conceptual framework
- ecopsychology views human health as tied to environment health - each side bidirectionally influences each other
- Colonizing societies have imposed western norms (anthropocentric, divide humanity from nature, and treat nature as subservient) which are antithetical to indigenous worldviews
The Cases
- indigenous identities are heterogenous, uniquely shaped by history and environment like any culture
- "The good life" is the idea that the earth can sustain all of life when humans live in balance with nature.
	- it recognizes the gifts of creation and bestows caretaking responsibilities upon human beings.
	- living in harmony with earth in a physical and spiritual sense
	- this view recognizes that nature has rights and is not a commodity
Mayangna 
- Forest loss in Bosawas reserve
	- faced environmental threats of increased development, resource extraction, and lack of enforcement of existing protective laws
	- their river is being polluted by industry
	- forests are clear-cut which impedes food acquisition
	- violence perpetuated by armed colonists
	- Bosawas is home to 3.5% of global biodiversity of flora and fauna and its preservation is crucial so some rare species
	- Colonists lay claim to large regions despite laws existing to protect it
- Relationship with the land
	- relationship with the land characterizes by peace, tranquility, and stewardship
	- private land ownership is a foreign concept
	- due to clear cutting, communities need to walk for days to be able to find animals to hunt
	- colonist presence has caused physical insecurity, psychological trauma, and anxiety for the Mayangna due to armed threats of violence
	- All the animals that have traditionally lived here are part of us because we have lived in harmony and they help us be better
nourished.
Anishinaabe
- water insecurity
	- community resides on 80km of shore on Lake Huron
	- migrated to the great lakes from Atlantic coast over 1000 years ago
	- Had traditional land-based economies and social structure reflected an intimate connection with and respect for nature
	- Treaties with the British crown restricted Anishinaabe settlements to the federal reserves that exist today, despite assimilation attempts
	- 150/600 first nations communities across Canada faced drinking water advisory warnings in 2017
	- Anishinaabe people feel responsibilities of stewardship to water that emanate from inherent rights and obligations to secure water for future generations
	- Water is spiritual and part of who they are
	- describe "the good life" as harmonious living with nature which involves taking care of water
	- The Anishinaabe's ability to rely on their surrounding water sources have slowly eroded as a result of direct impacts from historic agreements
		- loss of decision-making for on-reserve matters, and little involvement in water matters outside of reserve jurisdiction > deterioration of water quality
	- their relationship with water now involved fear and anxiety due to increase pollution, changing water levels, invasive species, and reduction of native plant and animal species which leads to unsustainability of traditional livelihood practices
	- Water is no longer cleaned and accessed naturally which impacts their spiritual connection to it, and are viewed as unhealthy forms of cleansing
Framework for transforming policy
- four interconnected dimensions for a decolonizing approach to policy reform 
	- ecological
		- the need to place the integrity of land and water above other priorities
		- governments and institutions regulate practices that threaten human-nature balances
	- sociopolitical
		- related to indigenous rights to self-determination
		- addresses broader range of social, political, and economic challenges that constrain communities for exercising their rights - involves representation
	- technical
		- finances and resources to meet community capacity needs
		- for water security: adequate facilities and infrastructure to provide safe water
		- wastewater services
	- educational
		- knowledge exchange of how important nature is to these people
