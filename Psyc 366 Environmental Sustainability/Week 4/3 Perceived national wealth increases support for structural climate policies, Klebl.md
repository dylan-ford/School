1. What is the difference between structural climate policies and individual-level behavior change policies? Why do the authors argue that structural policies are more effective? Why do the authors argue that it is important to identify the factors that shape people’s preferences for one type of policy over another?
	- Structural policies aim for systematic transformations through legislation, marketing incentives, and public investments. Individual-level policies focus on personal choices (recycling, energy saving)
	- structural policies are more effective because they change the physical and social contexts that constrain individual choices
	- Identifying factors that shape people's preferences for policies is important because time and resources are limited, and a shift towards individual-level policies may shift attention away from structural change and falsely imply that individual actions alone can save climate change
2. According to the study, why are wealthier countries often perceived as having a greater moral obligation to implement structural climate policies?
	- wealthier countries have a greater capacity to implement structural change and can more easily cushion short term costs. supported by the distributive justice norms (those with more should share) and the "ability-to-pay rule" in international negotiations
3. What are the key methods of studies 1 – 4? 
	1. What is the design of each study (e.g., correlational, experimental, …)? What did participants in each study do? 
			- studies 1 and 2 were correlational studies. study 3 was an experimental study using a fictional world. study 4 was a quasi-experimental study comparing real nations. in all studies participants chose between structural and individual policy proposals
	2. How did the authors measure structural vs individual level policy support?
		- support was measured on a slider scale where participants indicated a relative preference between two policy options regarding the same issue (1 structural the other individual)
	3. In Study 3, how did the researchers manipulate national wealth?
		- randomly assigned participants to a fictional country described either as the wealthiest, or poorest
4. What are the key results of studies 1 – 4 (i.e., main analyses and mediation analyses)?
	- across all studies, perceived national wealth positively predicted support for structural change policies
	- in studies 2 and 3 support was mediated by a perceived moral obligation for wealthy countries to contribute to the solution of global issues
5. Which two countries were compared in Study 4, and how did participants from these countries differ in their support for structural climate policies? Is this consistent with the findings from Study 3? What is the authors’ explanation for why?
	- study 4 compared the UK to south Africa. UK participants more strongly supported structural policies for their own country, south Africans preferred the wealthy country to implement structural change over their own. this is consistent with study 3; people view it as the "morally right thing to do " for a nation to implement change in proportion to its resources
