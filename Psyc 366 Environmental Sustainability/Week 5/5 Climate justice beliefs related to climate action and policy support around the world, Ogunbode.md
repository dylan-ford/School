1. What are three elements/ types of climate justice discussed in this paper, and how are they defined? (i.e., distributional, procedural and recognitional (in)justices)
	- Distributional: uneven distribution of vulnerability and adaptive capacity across social groups, often linked to to historical of colonization and unequal wealth 
	- Procedural: the under-representation of front line communities in the selection and implementation of climate response
	- Recognitional: the failure to recognize the rights and interests of marginalized groups as legitimate in policy-making
2. Methods:
	a. Describe the Climate Justice Beliefs Index (CJBI). What key dimensions of climate justice does it measure?
	- CJBI: a 9 item index measuring endorsement of ideas like the disproportionate impact on the poor, women and people of colour; the need for redistributing resources from the wealthy; the role of of capitalism and colonialism in driving the crisis 
	b. What is the difference between “climate justice beliefs” and “climate justice awareness” as measured in this study? (see “outcome measures” on page 8)
	- beliefs vs awareness: awareness: measures whether a participant had ever heard of the term "climate justice" before. belief: measures the actual endorsement of the concepts underlying the term
3. Results: Averaged across countries…
	a. What percentage of survey participants across the 11 countries had never heard of climate justice before the study? What does this suggest about public awareness?
	- 66.2% of participants had never heard of climate justice before the study. This suggests low conceptual awareness despite high underlying agreement with justice principles
	b. Which two beliefs on the CJBI were the most widely endorsed?
	- people in poverty suffer worse outcomes
	- people from most-affected communities should have the most say
	c. Which two beliefs on the CJBI had the lowest levels of endorsement? What is the authors’ explanation for why (in the discussion)?
	- climate change affects women more than men
	- impacts are worse for indigenous people and people of colour
	- these "culture war" topics are politically polarized so gender/racial injustice aren't apparent to most people
	d. How did climate justice beliefs in this study relate to engagement in climate action, online activism, PEB and support for climate change mitigation policies?
	- climate justice beliefs positively predicted engagement in climate action and support of mitigation policies 
4. According to the study, how do political orientation and education level relate to climate justice beliefs?
	- right-wing leaning individuals gave themselves higher self-ratings for knowledge but lower for endorsement of climate change beliefs
	- attaining a university degree was higher positively associated with knowledge, awareness, and CJBI
