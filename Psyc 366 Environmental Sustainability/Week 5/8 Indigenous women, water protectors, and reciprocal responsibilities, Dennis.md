• What are MEWW and NODAPL?
- Mother Earth Water Walks: an initiative led by Anishinaabe women in Canada to raise awareness of their spiritual connection to the water
- NoDAPL: refers to the movement against the Dakota Access Pipeline at the standing rock reservation

• According to the paper, how do gender relations differ in traditional Indigenous cultures in N.A., compared to dominant Canadian and American culture?
- traditionally indigenous cultures often had equitable social roles between men and women that promoted community balance. women held leadership positions, possessed great personal autonomy, and had their labour viewed equal to women. 
- contrasted are western civilizations which are heteropatriarchal and debase women's roles through colonization and market-centered economies
• According to the paper, what special responsibilities to Indigenous women have?
- they have a sacred duty to protect Earth's resources, act as keepers of traditional knowledge and safeguard water. responsible for collective community well-being and ensuring a healthy future for future generations
• What is collective continuance?
- a communities' ability to adapt sufficiently so that its members and livelihoods can flourish in the future
• How do Indigenous people think about and relate to water?
- water is viewed as a living entity with its own spirit and and responsibility. considered the "lifeblood" of mother earth and is intrinsically linked to women because the first environment for life is inside a woman's body
• What is the relationship between Indigenous women and resistance to environmentally damaging projects?
- Indigenous women have been at the forefront of environmental movements, framing their resistance not just as protest, but as an act of sovereignty and protection of Creation for all life. Their resistance is rooted in centuries of defending their families and lands from colonial injustice
• How do the authors define direct action in a broader way that it is usually conceptualized?
	The authors define direct actions as everyday acts of resurgence and "placing Indigenous bodies on the land in any Indigenous context through engagement with Indigenous practices." its an unapologetic practice of tradition that serves as a living alternative to colonial authority
• What do the authors suggest for social work practice (which can also apply to psyc practice, by the way)?
- follow the leadership of Indigenous women on the frontlines
- expand the definitions of "social" response to indlude interdependencies with the more-than-human world
- Support direct action and disruptive tactics as legitimate tools for justice
- Engage in meaningful solidarity, including divestment from institutions finding the mistreatment ofwater protectors and placing accompaniment by putting one's own body on the line
