Tragedy of the commons 1968 Garrett Hardin
- assumptions:
	- recognizing that there exist ecological limits
	- people act rationally in their individual self-interest
	- people see their own impact as trivial
	- when unsustainability is noticed, people tend to consume at high levels before others get what is left
	- no communication
- solutions
	- external coercion (government regulation/ownership)
	- privatization (end commons)
A social psychological critique
- humans don't always act as rational, self-interested individuals
	- humans have the capacity for altruism; helping on behalf of others
	- we are not just individuals
	- we are not entirely rational
- question becomes one of context and variation
	- communication increases cooperation in a commons dilemma setting
		- meta-analysis by Gifford and Hine (1997)
			- communication (r = .54)
		- Balliet (2010) meta-analysis
			- face-to-face discussion (r = .51)
			- written messages (r = .22)
	- even *imagined* communication increases cooperation
From Me to We
- how do we define ourselves
- collective identity facilitates cooperation
	- by creating a shared identity you shift peoples focus on prosocial behaviours
	- more likely to develop shared norms, develop a sense of trust, and develop expectations of reciprocity
		- also trust other people will uphold punishment for rulebreakers in addition to not wanting to let the group down
Cooperating with the future
- a line-up of successive groups - "generations"
	- extract a resource to exhaustion
	- or leave something for the next group
- experimental manipulation of how decisions are made: individual or by voting---everyone would be held to the results of the vote
	- when people were making individual decisions, within a few generations the resources would be exhausted
	- when making group decisions the resource was almost always sustained
		- majority constrains a minority of over-users
	- equal outcomes for current generation
Elinor Ostrom
- 2009 Nobel prize in economics
- governing the commons
- field work on successful and unsuccessful management of common-pool resources
- look at situations where people were dependent on their environment for their survival, specifically in situations without an outside governing body
	- Hardin's perspective would state that people are unable to share a resource over time amongst themselves
		- one example she looked at was irrigation systems in Spain where people had to irrigate farms based on river. their systems pulled water from the river; all people upstream who divert water leave less for people downstream
			- they found a system that worked
		- a fishery in Alanya, Turkey: 
			- in 1970s there was a lot of intercommunity conflict over access to fishing spots leading to overfishing
			- developed a cooperative which created a map of fishing hot spots, and people would be randomly assigned to a spot, and they would rotate
- successful self-governing commons have remained stable due to:
	- sharing a past and future
	- successive generations rely on passed down knowledge of the local ecology
	- norms for interdependence
		- sense of shared responsibility and future
	- existed with relative equality of social structure
	- most individuals affected by rules can participate in shaping them
		- self-governance allows for individual agency in changing rules
	- no external interference
	- systems for monitoring behaviour, rewards, and punishments
	- **local control through democratic, participatory systems**
	- important that Ostrom's observed scale for these phenomena was always small
