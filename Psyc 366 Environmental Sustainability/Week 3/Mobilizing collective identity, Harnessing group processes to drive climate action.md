Fielding, K.S., Pittaway, C., Louis, W., & Hornsey, M. (2026). Mobilizing collective identity: Harnessing group processes to drive climate action. In M. T. Schmitt, G. Anjum, S. Clayton, & C. Manning (Eds.), Psychology and climate change: Human perception

**Questions**
- How does social identity approach help us understand the different perspectives and actions of the 3 personas the authors introduce in the chapter (Tom, Linda, Caitlin)?
	- Tom: retires mining engineer and conservative - skepticism and opposition to climate policy linked to political identity: conservative affiliation strong predictor of climate skepticism
	- Linda: music teacher from Vanuatu, green party campaigner; climate change is front of mind for her due to identity with nation threatened by climate impact. belongs to groups where activism is a central norm
	- Caitlyn: landscape architect who believes in climate change but doesn't act. her professional and friendship group identities prioritize technological and design solutions over individual or activist responses
- What do the authors (and the social identity approach) mean by “collective identities”?
	- aspects of an individual's self-concept are derived from membership to groups
- Why is a collective analysis needed in understanding responses to climate change?
	-  need for collective analysis is necessary because climate responses map onto group identities
		- intergroup conflict drives much of the climate debate and transformative change requires coordinated collective effort
- What two theories comprise the social identity approach?
	- social identity theory: focuses on intergroup relations
	- self-categorization theory: focused on intragroup and intergroup processes
- What are the main assumptions of the social identity approach (pages 5-7)?
	- the self-concept includes both personal and collective identities
	- individuals will self-categorize based on whichever collective identity is made salient by the current context
	- self-categorization leads people to accentuate similarities with their ingroup and differences with outgroups
	- group members are guided by an ingroup prototype-the perceived set of beliefs and behaviours that define the group
	- people strive for positive distinctiveness, motivated to see their group as superior to others
- What are the processes proposed by the SIMPEA model that could explain whether people engaged in climate action? Make sure you know what SIMPEA stands for.
	- SIMPEA: social identity model of pro-environmental action: proposes appraisal of an environmental crisis leads to four key social identity processes:
		- ingroup identification
		- ingroup norms/goals
		- collective efficacy
		- collective emotions
- What are the processes involved in the Social Identity Model of Collective Action (SIMCA) and the Encapsulated Model of Social Identity in Collective Action (EMSICA)? How are the models different?
	- SIMCA: argues identification with politicized groups, group-based anger at injustice, and collective efficacy beliefs drive action; views emotions and efficacy as flowing from identity
	- EMSICA: similar to SIMCA but suggests strong emotions and efficacy beliefs can actually be the impetus for forming new group identity
- How does research support the idea that identification with groups predict climate action?
	- research shows 89% of studies link collective identity to pro-environmental behaviour
- What kind of groups are most likely to predict action?
	- identification with pro-climate activist groups or environment-related opinion-based groups is the strongest predictor of action
- Why might minority groups be less likely to identify with the climate change action groups? 
	- people of colour may feel unwelcome in climate movements dominated by white, middle-class participants
- What does this suggest the climate movement needs to do?
	- prioritize diversity and inclusiveness
- In what ways does research on norms and pro-environmental behaviour not always align with the social identity approach? From the social identity approach what kind of norms matter and when?
	- other models view norms as general social pressure, the social identity approach specifies only norms of a contextually salient ingroup guide behaviour
- What is the difference between injunctive and descriptive norms?
	- injunctive: what group members think should be done
	- descriptive: what is actually done
- Is there evidence that group norms are relevant to environmental and climate relevant behaviour?
	- meta-analyses show social norms significantly impact behaviours like energy conservation and solar adoption
- What happens when there is a misalignment between injunctive and descriptive norms?
	- if injunctive norms are high, but descriptive norms are low it can undermine action
- What happens when someone belongs to one group with norms favouring environmental actions, and another group that is anti-environmentalist?
	- the effect depends on their pre-existing attitudes, where strong pro-environment views may be energized to compensate for the "bad" group, while those with weak views may be demotivated
- What are collective efficacy beliefs and how are they relevant to climate action? What are participatory efficacy beliefs?
	- collective efficacy: belief that one's group can successfully mitigate climate change
	- participatory efficacy: belief that one's own effort specifically helps the group achieve its goal
- What is meant by “group-based emotions?”
	- emotions felt on behalf of one's group
- What are some of the types of group-based emotion that are related to environmentally relevant behaviour? What kind of emotions seem to work better for motivating action – positive or negative?
	- negative emotions (guilt and anger) are generally more effective motivators for action than positive ones like pride, which can lead to complacency
- How does the social identity approach help us understand conflict between groups around climate change?
	- recognizing individuals derive their self-concept from the groups they belong to and are motivated to defend the interests of
	- drivers of intergroup conflict: 
		- defense of interests and status
		- perceived illegitimacy
- How do groups with a vested interest in climate inaction create political conflict to delay climate action?
	- vested interests use group identities to manufacture uncertainty and polarization to delay policy
- How might climate conflict emerge along geographic lines?
	- transition policies can create us vs them divisions between urban and rural communities, especially of rural livelihoods (coal) are threatened
- How might climate change increase intergroup conflict or even cause new conflicts to emerge?
	- tipping points leading to food/water scarcity and climate refugees act as "threat multipliers" for future intergroup violence
- The authors describe 7 strategies suggested by the social identity approach for mobilizing climate action. **Know each of these strategies and be able to apply them to specific groups. Also be aware of potential complications the authors identify for implementing some of these strategies and how to avoid having them backfire.**
	1. Align identity with action
		- use identity leadership to embed climate action into the group's prototype
	2. use ingroup messengers
		- ensure messages come from prototypical members
	3. appeal to superordinate identities
		- use broad identities like "we humans" to overcome "us vs them" conflicts
	4. harness ingroup norms
		- communicate that the group approves of and practices green behaviour
	5. increase collective efficacy
		- highlight past group success and the feasibility of solutions
	6. elicit collective emotions
		- remind the group of its responsibility for danger to trigger collectives guilt
	7. affirm moral convictions
		- focus on shared values when the situation is difficult
- How do you best use ingroup norms to influence behaviour if injunctive norms are supportive buy descriptive norms are not?
	- if descriptive norms are low but injunctive are high, use dynamic norms to indicate a trend toward improvement
- What are some of the ways to increase a sense of efficacy in the environmental context?
	- use non-analytical cues like imagery of people taking action or provide environmental cognitive alternatives
- What might motivate action when efficacy is low, descriptive norms are unsupportive, and behavior is costly?
	- motivation in bleak times: when efficacy is low and behaviour is costly, people are motivated by shared moral convictions
