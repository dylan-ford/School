Research on social norms
- Injunctive norms: what we think we should do; what do people in a group think we should do; how we ought to act
- Descriptive norm: perceptions of what people actually do

What to do if norms aren't pro-environmental
- Some norms might be more pro-environmental than we think - correct misperceptions
- Beware of conflicting injunctive and descriptive norms.
- Some norms are changing – let people know
- As norms are changing, if we let people know it can encourage them to adopt new behaviour
- Challenge and change norms

Dynamic norms
- Study where people were given bogus feedback about how norms will affect behaviour - participants waiting in line to buy lunch 

Minority influence
- More effective if not seen as self-interested (person is just saying a thing because its self beneficial)
- More effective if consistent; unwavering
- Idea is to get people to rethink their own beliefs because the person with the minority belief is so confident/fervent
- Positive effect of individual behaviour is amplified by social influence

The costs of inconsistency
- Participants watched a video debate between 2 people talking about recycling
- Anti-recycling speaker and pro-recycling speaker who is either:
- Described neutrally or as an environmentalist (second condition comes with expectations from the viewer)
- In the condition where there was a confrontation (by the recycler to the anti-recycler) the recycling intention of viewers positively, but in the no confrontation condition recycling intention decreased (because person who is perceived as an environmentalist would be thought of to advocate for their cause, and by not doing so it makes the viewer question the stance)  

Social identity approach
- One of the fundamental assumptions is that people define themselves as individuals but also at the collective level (social identity)    
- What is self defining consists of what groups we perceive we belong to    
- Collective identities are relational   
	- Groups can harm or benefit each other    
	- Groups vary in status and power    
	- Judgements of fairness are an important predictor of intergroup behaviour 
- Identity varies with context
	- We think of ourselves as belonging to a single group identity at a time, whichever is most salient/meaningful at a given time
	- We identify with some groups more than others; groups that are more meaningful to us are more likely to influence our behaviour and values
	- When we self-define at collective level:
		- Accentuation: emphasize the things we have in common with group members, and exaggerate the things that makes our group distinct from others
		- Increases conformity
		- Motivation to support group interests and values
		- Can lead to conflict (not always bad)

Social identity strategies
- Align identity with action
	- Make pro-environmental identities salient
	- Make salient identities more pro-environmental (involves connection to existing group values)
- Promote action through ingroup messages
	- Promote action in our own ingroups
- Appeal to a superordinate identity
	- Useful when a group is in conflict with another group
	- Gets people to think about things at a level higher than the conflict to supersede it and find commonality
- Communicate pro-environmental ingroup norms
	- If descriptive and injunctive are both supportive
	- If descriptive norms are not supportive, consider dynamic norms
- Increase collective efficacy
	- Give people in a group a sense that working together can actually make a difference
- Elicit collective emotions: anger, fear, guilt*** (needs more research)
- Affirm ingroup moral convictions
	- Make acting something that's a moral obligation
