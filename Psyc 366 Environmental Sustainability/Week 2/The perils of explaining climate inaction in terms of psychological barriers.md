Schmitt, M.T., Neufeld, S.D., Mackay, C.M.L., & Dys-Steenbergen, O. (2022). The perils of
explaining climate inaction in terms of psychological barriers. Journal of Social Issues, 76,
123-135.

**Questions**
- Summarize the main point of the paper in one sentence.
	- the paper argues that explaining climate inaction as a result of inherent psychological barriers misrepresents human nature as static and obscures the influence of social structure context and power dynamics.
	- This risks undermining the possibility of transformative social change
- What approach do the authors take in this paper?
	- the authors take a critical social psychology approach: which entails rejecting individualized "atomized" views of human behaviour, emphasizing instead that psychological processes are fundamentally contextual and social in nature
- What are the main assumptions of this approach that are used in this paper? (spread throughout sections)
	- **social contextuality**: no psychological processes exist exclusively in the mind, they are produced by narratives within historical, structural, and social contexts
	- **power and inequality**: systems are not neutral; elite individuals and institutions have the power to create laws, structures, and discourses that serve their interests and maintain the status quo
	- co-construction of reality: psychological theory isn't a commentary on the world but is part of it
		- the narratives scientists create shape human self-understanding and what people believe is possible
	- human plasticity: human nature is complex and multifaceted rather than fixed; psychological responses are contingent upon the environment rather than being immutable species-wide deficits
- How do these authors critique the idea of the ancient brain as a psychological barrier?
	- ancient brain suggests the human brain is wired to ignore climate change as it can only process immediate, local danger
	- **capacity for foresight**: humans are demonstrably capable of caring about the future and distant others, such as when simple reminders of one's legacy increase climate concern
	- **social vs psychological barrier** the failure to act on long-term threats is not an inherent brain deficit but a social failure to create contexts that encourage long-term thinking
	- **context dependency**: the degree to which any evolved tendency manifests depend on the social-structural context, not just biology
- How do these authors critique the idea of free-market ideology as a psychological barrier?
	- elite construction: neoliberalism is not a spontaneous psychological state; it is an ideology deliberately promoted by elites to justify policies that serve their economic interests
	- structural reality: the barrier is often structural rather than mental; neoliberal policies have gutted environmental regulations and are tied to a capitalist system that requires continuous economic growth-a reality that conflicts with a finite planet
	- intentional misinformation: attributing skepticism to individual "system justification motives" ignores the massive financial investment fossil fuel companies have made to intentionally seed doubt and misinform the public
	- individualization of responsibility: framing ideology as a psychological barrier shifts the focus to individual consumer choices, which obscures the role of powerful institutions and the need for collective political action to change the distribution of power
