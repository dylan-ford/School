Gifford, R. (2011). The dragons of inaction: Psychological barriers that limit climate change
mitigation. American Psychologist, 66, 290-302.

**Questions**
- Summarize the main point of the whole paper in one sentence.
	- While structural barriers are significant, individuals are primarily hindered from taking necessary climate action by seven categories of psychological barriers, known as dragons of inaction
- Name and describe these “dragons of inaction”:
	- Ancient brain
		- Human brains have not evolved to competently contest with existential threats like climate change. the modern world is difficult to process
		- they remain specialized for dealing with immediate dangers, local groups, and exploitable resources
	- Ideologies or worldviews that can undermine action (4 types)
		- **Worldviews**: broad belief systems can clash with the need for collective action (capitalism)
		- **suprahuman powers**: belief that a deity or mother nature will provide for humans or take a course we cannot influence > leads to personal inaction
		- **technosalvation**: overconfident faith that mechanical innovation alone will solve the climate crisis > barrier to an individual's behavioural change
		- system justification: tendency to justify and defend the societal status quo to maintain a comfortable lifestyle > refusal to adopt mitigative actions that disturb social harmony
	- Environmental Numbness
		- a lack of awareness regarding the physical environment, often because climate change is not causing immediate personal difficulties
		- or a numbness that occurs when people hear the same climate messages too often
	- Behavioral momentum
		- habit; tendency for established behavioural patterns to be extremely resistant to change, require a substantial push to redirect
	- Perceived Inequity
		- justification for inaction based on the belief that others are not cooperating, leading to a fear of being a "sucker" while "free-riders" take advantage of the situations
	- Perceived behavioural control / self-efficacy
		- the sense that an individual has little power to influence the outcome of a global problem or that their specific actions will not have a meaningful impact
	- Tokenism
		- the tendency to adopt easy, low-cost pro-environmental actions (recycling) that have little impact on greenhouse gas emissions, often choosing them over more effective but higher-cost behavioural changes
