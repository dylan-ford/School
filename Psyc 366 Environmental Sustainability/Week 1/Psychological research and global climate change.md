Clayton, S., Devine-Wright, P., Stern, P. C., Whitmarsh, L., Carrico, A., Steg, L., et al. (2015). Psychological research and global climate change. Nature Climate Change, 5, 640–646.

Abstract
- human behaviour is causing climate change but also integral for remedying it
- therefore psychological research should inform efforts to address climate change, avoid misunderstandings abbot human behaviour and motivations that can lead to ineffective or misguided policies
- 3 key research areas
	- human perceptions of climate change
	- understanding which individual and household behaviours  drive climate change and mitigation responses
	- impact of climate change on human well-being and adaptation responses
- perception of climate change and factors influencing mitigation and adaptation behaviours are crucial to responding to climate change effectively as a society
	- too much policy is based on oversimplification - assuming that simply informing people about climate change is sufficient to change behaviour

Public perceptions of climate change
- how we learn information diversifies understanding; the sources of our information should be examined
	- direct experiences of events related to climate change are more powerful than second-hand information in informing attitudes and behaviours
	- e.g. people who believed global warming isn't happening were less likely to remember that they had experienced a warmer-than-usual summer during the previous year; thus the experience on perceptions of climate change is moderated by other factors
- **Climate change communication.**
	- for messages to be attended and responded to, sources must be have 3 qualities:
		- sources must be trusted and attractive
		- the message must be relevant, clear, and coherent
		- the audience must be motivated to act
	- A major predictor of climate change belief is political ideology, demonstrating that this is a function of group identity and not a shared belief system
- **Motivational processes and cognitive biases**
	- discomfort from the political, ethical, and social implications of climate change can lead to climate messages to be rejected
	- identities (political) provide people with mental modes that shape their understandings and motivate particular interpretations of information; shapes the lens through which people filter information
		- thus rejection of a scientific consensus can be linked to the perceived threat to one's current way of life
	- influence of availability heuristic
	- other influences on perception of risks and threaten people's motivation to respond:
		- overly optimistic
		- focus on present rather than future
		- perceptions of risk are conditioned by mental shortcuts, emotions, and life experience factors
	- misperceptions persist because people seek information that aligns with their beliefs
Behavioural drivers and mitigation responses
- psychology complements economic models by identifying non-financial factors that drive or hinder actions to reduce greenhouse gas emissions
- reducing use of energy-consuming technologies
	- information about personal usage vs others can reduced consumption but can increase use if they are below their neighbours
		- information has greater impact on behaviour if its tailored to a personal situation so it resonates with peoples' values
	- In general, people are more likely to engage in energy-use reduction when they have strong self-transcendence values, self-identity as pro-environmental, are aware of problems caused by their behaviour, feel morally obliged to engage in energy-saving behaviours, have favourable attitudes towards energy savings, feel capable of engaging in these behaviours, and have a social context that supports their behaviours
- inducing investment in low-emissions technologies
	- financial incentives are effective but disappear once an incentive is removed; changes to values/intrinsic factors provide longer term change
	- households often underinvest in low-carbon technologies even when they would save money
		- often cause by factors like lack of trust in organizations or high level of effort to access incentives
	- public opposition to energy projects is frequently rooted in "place attachment" - an emotional bond with a location
		- policies are more likely to be accepted if they're perceived as fair, reward positive behaviour rather than punish high energy use, and align with publics moral obligations
Impact on human well-being and adaptation responses
- climate change affects psychological health through sudden disasters and gradual environmental shifts
	- PTSD, anxiety, depression from extreme weather
	- stress like food insecurity and loss of livelihood from long term shifts
- people underestimate the likelihood of being affected by disasters 
	- place-based identities can make individuals less willing to learn new skills or relocate even in the face of unsustainable weather
- successful adaptation depends on social factors like trust and collectives identity
	- research shows that group discussions and providing specific, personal action can improve community preparedness and cooperation

**Questions**
- What factors shape people’s perceptions of climate change? What is the role of sources of information in people’s beliefs?
	- factors shaping perception of climate change:
		- how information is communicated and by whom
			- direct experience is the most powerful source of information
			- the source of the information must be trusted and attractive and the message must be relevant, clear, and coherent
			- the audience must be motivated to act, and focused, else they will pay more attention to peripheral cues of the communicator
			- mass media is the primary source of information, and people tend to accept view from trusted and attitudinally reinforcing sources and ignore those that challenge existing views
- Explain the complicated relationship between political orientation and how people respond to climate change science and communication.
	- political ideology is a major predictor of climate change belief which demonstrates this belief is more a result of group identity and a need for belonging rather than a well-considered idea
		- right wing politics that emphasize individual autonomy are more likely to reject mainstream climate science
	- people tie their identities to their political beliefs, which they use as a tool to navigate the world, and when information contradicts their worldview (these implicit navigation tools) people become resistant to the information because it threatens their worldview
- How do motivational processes and biases affect perception of climate change?
	- people interpret climate information through their values and identities
	- identities provide people with tools to navigate the world and information that contradicts an identity is likely to be discarded
	- confirmation bias
	- risk perceptions are influenced by availability heuristic; recent personal experiences are most influential
	- optimism bias
- How much of our climate-related behavioural decisions are made with conscious awareness?
	- a great deal of environmentally relevant behaviour is driven by forces such as norms, habits, and default options
- How well do financial decisions work in motivating climate change mitigating behaviour?
	- level of effort to take advantage of an incentive and trust in institutions can impact people's willingness to adopt new energy efficient technology 
	- effects of financial inducements last only while the incentives are in place
	- focusing attention on financial gain shifts focus from "doing good" to "gaining a profit"
