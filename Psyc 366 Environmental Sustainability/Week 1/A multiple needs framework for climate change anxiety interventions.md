Bingley, W. J., Tran, A., Boyd, C. P., Gibson, K., Kalokerinos, E. K., Koval, P., Kashima, Y., McDonald, D., & Greenaway, K. H. (2022). A multiple needs framework for climate change anxiety interventions. American Psychologist, 77(7), 812–821.

- psychological interventions to address climate change anxiety have unintended effects on outcomes other than individual well being
	- such as group cohesion and pro-environmental behavior
- multiple needs framework is used to analyze interventions in terms of their individual, social, and environmental outcomes
	- identifies interventions focused on: problem-focused action, emotion management, and enhancing social connections
- climate change anxiety: anxiety or worry about climate change and its effects; a chronic fear of environmental doom
	-  47% of us adults say climate change related stress effects their daily lives detrimentally, with this % increasing amongst adults of nations that are more at risk

Multiple needs framework:
- proposes climate change anxiety interventions differ in their effects on individual, social and environmental outcomes
	- interventions should be evaluated on the effect they have on each outcome
- Individual needs
	- individuals may want to reduce the discomfort they feel from anxiety - hedonic motive
	- climate change anxiety can interfere with daily functioning - personal water consumption leading to death of millions
		- severe guilt
	- interventions may address individual's needs for hedonic well-being
- Social needs
	- goals of groups and social structures
	- social needs can be met by factors that shape people's willingness to work on their group's behalf
	- these interventions are potent when involving collective action - common purpose increases group cohesion
		- fostering relatedness, encouraging per interactions, increasing social connection
	- Social needs interventions may address social needs by promoting outcomes of interpersonal closeness, group cohesion, and societal interdependence
- Environmental needs
	- interventions may address local and global environmental needs by affecting pro-environmental attitudes and behaviours
	- interventions that strengthen people's connection to environmental activism are more likely to increase pro-environmental behaviour - impact pro-environmental norms
	- **interventions that promote connection to nature are also likely to promote pro-environmental behaviour**

**Questions**
- What are the multiple needs addressed in this paper?
	- individual, social, and environmental
	- explain each
- How did the authors of this paper attempt to assess the effectiveness of different ways of reducing climate anxiety?
	- for each type of intervention, the authors evaluated where it was likely to have a beneficial, neutral, or detrimental impact on individual, social, and environmental outcomes based on a set of criteria
		- based on the direction of the reported effect vs the anticipated desirability of the outcome
		- ex an intervention that increased pro-environmental behaviour would be considered beneficial to environmental needs
	- aims of this paper were to:
		- scope literature for climate change interventions
		- asses each in terms of ability to address individual, social, and environmental needs, 
		- identify interventions that address the broadest range of needs
		- identify gaps in literature
- According to the authors, which intervention strategies seem to work best at addressing multiple needs?
	- interventions most likely to meet all three needs were those centered around problem-focused action, emotion management, and fostering social connection
	- Problem focused action
		- interventions that involve engaging in action to address climate change - activism
		- collective action improve individual and environmental outcomes and maybe social outcomes
	- Emotion management
		- interventions focused on emotion management were: **individual or group emotion expression, engaging with and processing emotions, and developing emotional skills**
		- these interventions may be effecting at reducing **individual** anxiety
		- validating climate concerns/emotions helps reduce anxiety; social norms to suppress anxiety increase it
			- also reduces conflict and create social cohesiveness which improves **social outcomes**
	- Social connection
		- increased social support and community building
			- reduce individual CC anxiety and increase interconnectedness
		- social connection interventions also address environmental outcomes in that they involve pro-environmental action
- Which intervention strategies show mixed success?
	- Interventions that increase connection with nature can reduce or increase anxiety
		- connection to nature increases subjective well-being, it also highlights the effects of climate change which can be distressing to those who feel close to it
	- Meaning-focused coping (positive reappraisal) was associated with reduced anxiety
	- Problem-focused coping (focusing on ways to address climate change) was associated with increased anxiety
	- Psychological adaptation interventions (PAI) (increasing attentiveness, acceptance of reality, adopting problem-solving attitude) may have counterproductive effects on **individual well-being** outcomes
		- PAI predicted increased pro-environmental behaviour but increased climate change distress
- How well did connection to nature work as an intervention according to this paper?
	- Connection to nature interventions demonstrated mixed efficacy as a connection to nature was found to generally increase well-being for individuals, but an increase in that connection also highlighted the impact of climate change, which was distressing for those who felt particularly connected
